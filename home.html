What is going on here?

 The Linear Algebra calculator on this webpage is a way of looking at the subject algorithmically. As students of computer science, this is the view we take of nearly everything. As for the computer, it is the only view.


 The interest in the algorigthms is twofold; one is a matter of teaching, the other is a matter of learning. In mathematics it is good to have an intuition as to what you are doing, and Dr. Davis has done yeoman's work trying to give all of us this intuition. The computer has no such luxury (of Dr. Davis or intuition.) It can only take a set of numbers and a sequence of instructions and execute the given instructions on the given numbers. As a matter of teaching, this makes the teacher's job a little more difficult because computers do not know what you mean. They do exactly what you tell them to do. And to tell this stupid box what you want it to do, you have to tell it in a language it understands, which is of course not English. In an essay titled In the Beginning... Was the Command Line, Neal Stephenson said, "Back in the days of the command-line interface, users ... had to convert their thoughts into alphanumeric symbols and type them in, a grindingly tedious process that stripped away all ambiguity, laid bare all hidden assumptions, and cruelly punished laziness and imprecision."

 This gets us to the matter of learning. It is rare to teach something without learning in the process. This is just as true whether teaching computers or people. The process Stephenson wrote about, removing ambiguity and implicit assumptions, does a great deal in the way of clarifying thought and truly understanding the process you are attempting to explain. This is, incidently, the genesis of this project. The process of multiplying two matrices was confusing to me until I sat in front of my code editor and wrote the Python code seen on the matrix multiplication page. After that, it is nearly impossible to be confused about the process.
